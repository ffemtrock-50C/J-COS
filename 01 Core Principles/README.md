# Core Principles

> *"Standards define what we do. Principles explain why we do it."*

---

## Purpose

The Core Principles of J-COS define the philosophy that guides every decision, workflow, standard, and template within the system.

Unlike procedures, principles are intended to remain stable over time. While tools, technology, and organizational structures evolve, sound judgment is rooted in enduring ideas.

Every standard in J-COS should be traceable to one or more of these principles.

---

# Principles

## 1. Evidence Over Opinion

Decisions should be grounded in verifiable evidence whenever possible.

Opinions are valuable, but they should not be mistaken for facts.

When evidence is incomplete, uncertainty should be acknowledged rather than ignored.

---

## 2. Separate Facts from Assumptions

Facts are supported by evidence.

Assumptions are beliefs awaiting confirmation.

Both are useful.

Neither should be confused with the other.

---

## 3. Externalize Thinking

Important decisions should not rely solely on memory.

Capture reasoning, alternatives, assumptions, and lessons learned so they can be reviewed, improved, and shared.

---

## 4. Decision Latency

Make decisions as early as necessary—but no earlier.

Gather information while time permits.

Act decisively when additional delay no longer improves the quality of the decision.

---

## 5. Institutional Memory

Organizations should not depend upon the memory of individuals.

Capture knowledge so future leaders begin with understanding rather than rediscovery.

---

## 6. Second-Order Thinking

Every decision produces consequences beyond the immediate outcome.

Consider not only what will happen next, but what happens because of what happens next.

---

## 7. Reversible vs. Irreversible Decisions

Not all decisions carry the same weight.

Reversible decisions favor speed and experimentation.

Irreversible decisions demand greater deliberation.

Match the decision process to the cost of being wrong.

---

## 8. Humility Under Uncertainty

Confidence and certainty are not the same.

Acknowledge uncertainty.

Remain willing to revise conclusions when new evidence materially changes the situation.

---

## Living Philosophy

The principles contained within this directory are intended to evolve through experience.

New principles should emerge only when they describe enduring patterns of sound judgment rather than isolated techniques or temporary practices.

If a proposed principle would remain valuable twenty years from now, it likely belongs here.
